The temptation in the first month of an LLM project is to spend it tuning
prompts. The feedback is immediate, the iteration loop is tight, and the sense
of progress is real. It is also, in most cases, the wrong first investment.

## The hand-label detour

A faster first move is to collect twenty-five traces of the current pipeline
against real queries, print them out — or at minimum read them end-to-end, one
at a time — and label each one on two axes: did it get the right answer, and if
not, where upstream did the failure begin. This takes a few hours. It feels
slow. It produces the single artefact that every later decision depends on: a
small, concrete, domain-specific taxonomy of failure modes.

Without that taxonomy, every subsequent tuning decision is guided by the prompt
engineer's intuition about which failure matters most. With it, tuning
decisions are cheap because the priority ordering is visible.

## Why the taxonomy outlives the pipeline

Prompts change. Models change. Retrievers change. The taxonomy changes only
when the failure modes change — which is much less often. A taxonomy written in
week one, tuned in week two, and validated against a fresh batch of traces in
week four is often stable enough to carry the project to production.

Later, the same taxonomy becomes the label schema for an LLM-judge eval. The
cost of building the judge goes down dramatically when the categories are
already named, because the hard work of defining what "good" means has been
done.

## The one-hour rule

A practical floor: no more than one hour per week on prompt tuning until the
first twenty-five traces have been labeled. If that rule feels constraining,
that is because it is supposed to. The constraint is not a preference — it is a
forcing function to move the slowest, most valuable work up the schedule.
